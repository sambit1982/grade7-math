🔢 Algebra

What is Algebra?

Algebra is a branch of mathematics that uses letters (called variables) to represent unknown numbers. Instead of saying "some number plus 3 equals 7", we write:

x + 3 = 7

Here, x is the variable — it stands for the number we need to find. Algebra helps us describe patterns, solve problems, and understand relationships between quantities.

Order of Operations (BEDMAS)

When a math expression has multiple operations, we follow a specific order. In Canada we use BEDMAS:

B

Brackets first

E

Exponents

DM

Division & Multiplication (left to right)

AS

Addition & Subtraction (left to right)

Example: Evaluate 3 + 4 × 2

Multiply first: 4 × 2 = 8

Then add: 3 + 8 = 11 ✓

Common mistake: doing 3 + 4 = 7 first gives the wrong answer of 14!

Example: Evaluate (5 + 3)² ÷ 4 − 1

Brackets: (5 + 3) = 8

Exponents: 8² = 64

Division: 64 ÷ 4 = 16

Subtraction: 16 − 1 = 15 ✓

Expressions vs Equations

Expression

A math phrase without an equals sign. You can simplify it but not "solve" it.

2x + 5
3a − 7 + 2a

Equation

A statement that two expressions are equal. You can solve for the unknown.

2x + 5 = 11
3a − 7 = 8

Like Terms & Simplifying

Like terms have the same variable raised to the same power. You can combine them by adding or subtracting their coefficients.

Like terms: 3x and 5x → both have "x"

Not like terms: 3x and 5x² → different powers

Not like terms: 3x and 5y → different variables

Example: Simplify 4x + 3y − 2x + 7y

Group like terms: (4x − 2x) + (3y + 7y)

= 2x + 10y

Example: Simplify 6a + 5 − 2a + 3

Group: (6a − 2a) + (5 + 3)

= 4a + 8

The Distributive Property

To remove brackets, multiply the outside term by each term inside the brackets:

a(b + c) = ab + ac

Example: Expand 3(x + 4)

= 3 × x + 3 × 4

= 3x + 12

Example: Expand and simplify 2(3x − 1) + 4x

= 6x − 2 + 4x

= 10x − 2

Solving One-Step Equations

To solve an equation, isolate the variable by doing the opposite operation on both sides. Whatever you do to one side, you must do to the other!

Addition → Subtract: Solve x + 4 = 10

Subtract 4 from both sides: x = 10 − 4

x = 6 ✓

Multiplication → Divide: Solve 3x = 18

Divide both sides by 3: x = 18 ÷ 3

x = 6 ✓

Division → Multiply: Solve x/5 = 4

Multiply both sides by 5: x = 4 × 5

x = 20 ✓

Solving Two-Step Equations

Sometimes you need two steps: first undo addition/subtraction, then undo multiplication/division.

Example: Solve 2x + 3 = 11

Step 1: Subtract 3 from both sides → 2x = 8

Step 2: Divide both sides by 2 → x = 4 ✓

Example: Solve 5x − 7 = 18

Step 1: Add 7 to both sides → 5x = 25

Step 2: Divide both sides by 5 → x = 5 ✓

Example: Solve (x/3) + 2 = 6

Step 1: Subtract 2 → x/3 = 4

Step 2: Multiply by 3 → x = 12 ✓

Equations with Variables on Both Sides

When the variable appears on both sides, move all variable terms to one side first.

Example: Solve 5x + 2 = 3x + 10

Step 1: Subtract 3x from both sides → 2x + 2 = 10

Step 2: Subtract 2 → 2x = 8

Step 3: Divide by 2 → x = 4 ✓

Check: 5(4) + 2 = 22 and 3(4) + 10 = 22 ✓

Inequalities

An inequality is like an equation, but instead of "=" we use comparison symbols:

< means "less than"
> means "greater than"
≤ means "less than or equal to"
≥ means "greater than or equal to"

Solve inequalities the same way as equations, with one important rule:

⚠️ When you multiply or divide by a negative number, FLIP the inequality sign!

Example: Solve 2x + 1 < 9

Subtract 1: 2x < 8

Divide by 2: x < 4

This means x can be any number less than 4 (like 3, 2, 0, −5, etc.)

Word Problems → Equations

The key skill is translating English into math. Look for clue words:

"is" → =

"more than" / "sum" → +

"less than" / "difference" → −

"times" / "product" → ×

"divided by" / "quotient" → ÷

"a number" → x

Problem: "Three more than twice a number is 17. Find the number."

Translate: 2x + 3 = 17

Solve: 2x = 14 → x = 7 ✓

Problem: "A movie ticket costs $12. Popcorn costs $p. You spend $19 total. How much was the popcorn?"

Equation: 12 + p = 19

Solve: p = 19 − 12 = $7 ✓

📐 Geometry

Types of Lines

Parallel lines (∥) run in the same direction and never intersect. Think of railway tracks!

Perpendicular lines (⊥) intersect at exactly 90°. Think of the corner of a book!

Types of Angles

An angle is formed when two rays share a common endpoint (the vertex).

A reflex angle is greater than 180° but less than 360°. A full rotation is exactly 360°.

Angle Relationships

Complementary angles add up to 90°. Think: they complete a right angle.

∠A + ∠B = 90°

If one angle is 35°, its complement is 90° − 35° = 55°

Supplementary angles add up to 180°. Think: they make a straight line.

∠A + ∠B = 180°

If one angle is 110°, its supplement is 180° − 110° = 70°

Vertically opposite angles are formed when two lines cross. They are always equal.

Angles on a straight line add up to 180°. Angles around a point add up to 360°.

Triangles — Classified by Sides

A triangle has 3 sides and 3 angles that always add up to 180° (the Angle Sum Property).

Triangles — Classified by Angles

Angle Sum Property: ∠A + ∠B + ∠C = 180°

Example: A triangle has angles 45° and 65°. Find the third angle.

Third angle = 180° − 45° − 65° = 70° ✓

Important Triangle Theorems

1. Exterior Angle Theorem: An exterior angle of a triangle equals the sum of the two non-adjacent interior angles.

If interior angles are 50° and 70°, the exterior angle = 50° + 70° = 120°

2. Triangle Inequality Theorem: The sum of any two sides must be greater than the third side.

a + b > c    AND    a + c > b    AND    b + c > a

Can sides 3, 4, 8 form a triangle?

3 + 4 = 7, but 7 < 8. No! These cannot form a triangle.

Can sides 3, 4, 5 form a triangle?

3 + 4 = 7 > 5 ✓   3 + 5 = 8 > 4 ✓   4 + 5 = 9 > 3 ✓   Yes!

3. Isosceles Triangle Theorem: If two sides of a triangle are equal, the angles opposite those sides are also equal.

Circle Properties

Key parts:

• Radius (r) — distance from centre to edge

• Diameter (d) — distance across through the centre (d = 2r)

• Chord — a line segment connecting two points on the circle

• Arc — a portion of the circumference

• Sector — a "pizza slice" (area between two radii and an arc)

• π (pi) ≈ 3.14159... — the ratio of circumference to diameter for every circle!

Circumference = 2πr = πd
Area = πr²

Example: A circle has radius 7 cm. Find its circumference and area.

Circumference = 2 × π × 7 = 14π ≈ 43.98 cm

Area = π × 7² = 49π ≈ 153.94 cm²

Quadrilaterals (4-Sided Shapes)

All quadrilaterals have interior angles that add up to 360°.

Square: A = s²  |  P = 4s
Rectangle: A = l × w  |  P = 2(l + w)
Parallelogram: A = base × height
Trapezoid: A = ½ × (a + b) × height   (a, b are the parallel sides)

Polygons

A polygon is a closed shape with straight sides. Here are the common ones:

• Triangle — 3 sides

• Quadrilateral — 4 sides

• Pentagon — 5 sides

• Hexagon — 6 sides

• Heptagon — 7 sides

• Octagon — 8 sides (stop sign!)

• Nonagon — 9 sides

• Decagon — 10 sides

A regular polygon has all sides equal and all angles equal.

Sum of interior angles = (n − 2) × 180°
Each angle of a regular polygon = (n − 2) × 180° ÷ n

Example: Find the sum of interior angles of a hexagon (n = 6).

Sum = (6 − 2) × 180° = 4 × 180° = 720°

Each angle of a regular hexagon = 720° ÷ 6 = 120°

Symmetry & Transformations

Line symmetry: A shape has line symmetry if you can fold it along a line and both halves match exactly.

• A square has 4 lines of symmetry

• A rectangle has 2 lines of symmetry

• An equilateral triangle has 3 lines of symmetry

• A circle has infinite lines of symmetry

Rotational symmetry: A shape has rotational symmetry if it looks the same after being rotated less than 360°. The order is how many times it matches in a full turn.

A square has rotational symmetry of order 4 (matches at 90°, 180°, 270°, 360°)

An equilateral triangle has rotational symmetry of order 3 (matches at 120°, 240°, 360°)

🧊 3D Shapes

What are 3D Shapes?

3D (three-dimensional) shapes have length, width, and height. Unlike flat 2D shapes, they take up space and have volume.

Key vocabulary:

• Face — a flat surface of a 3D shape

• Edge — where two faces meet (a line)

• Vertex — a corner point where edges meet (plural: vertices)

Euler's Formula: Faces + Vertices − Edges = 2

Cube: 6 faces + 8 vertices − 12 edges = 2 ✓

This formula works for all convex polyhedra (3D shapes with flat faces)!

Prisms

A prism has two identical parallel bases connected by rectangular faces. It's named after the shape of its base.

Volume of any prism = Base Area × height
Surface Area = 2 × Base Area + Perimeter of base × height

Example: A rectangular prism (box) is 5 cm × 3 cm × 4 cm.

Volume = 5 × 3 × 4 = 60 cm³

Surface Area = 2(5×3 + 5×4 + 3×4) = 2(15 + 20 + 12) = 94 cm²

🌍 Real world: A cereal box, a brick, a shipping container — all rectangular prisms! A Toblerone box is a triangular prism.

Pyramids

A pyramid has one base and triangular faces that meet at a single point (the apex).

Volume of a pyramid = (1/3) × Base Area × height

Example: A square pyramid has base side 6 cm and height 10 cm.

Base Area = 6² = 36 cm²

Volume = (1/3) × 36 × 10 = 120 cm³

🌍 Real world: The Great Pyramid of Giza in Egypt! Its base is about 230 m on each side and it's 146 m tall.

Volume ≈ (1/3) × 230² × 146 ≈ 2,574,467 m³ — that's massive!

Cylinder

A cylinder has two circular bases connected by a curved surface. Think of a soup can!

Volume = πr²h
Surface Area = 2πr² + 2πrh

Example: A can has radius 4 cm and height 12 cm.

Volume = π × 4² × 12 = 192π ≈ 603.19 cm³

Surface Area = 2π(4²) + 2π(4)(12) = 32π + 96π = 128π ≈ 402.12 cm²

🌍 Real world: Pop cans, water pipes, candles, tree trunks, and toilet paper rolls are all cylinders!

Sphere

A sphere is a perfectly round 3D shape — every point on its surface is the same distance from the centre.

Volume = (4/3)πr³
Surface Area = 4πr²

Example: A basketball has radius 12 cm.

Volume = (4/3) × π × 12³ = (4/3) × π × 1728 = 2304π ≈ 7,238.23 cm³

Surface Area = 4 × π × 12² = 576π ≈ 1,809.56 cm²

🌍 Real world: Basketballs, soccer balls, oranges, the Earth (approximately), and bubbles!

Cone

A cone has one circular base and a curved surface that tapers to a point (the apex).

Volume = (1/3)πr²h
Surface Area = πr² + πrl   (l = slant height)
Slant height: l = √(r² + h²)

Example: An ice cream cone has radius 3 cm and height 10 cm.

Volume = (1/3) × π × 3² × 10 = 30π ≈ 94.25 cm³

Slant height = √(9 + 100) = √109 ≈ 10.44 cm

🌍 Real world: Ice cream cones, traffic cones, party hats, and volcano shapes!

Nets of 3D Shapes

A net is a 2D pattern that folds up into a 3D shape. Imagine cutting a box along its edges and unfolding it flat — that's a net!

Nets help us calculate surface area by finding the area of each flat face.

Cube net: 6 identical squares arranged in a cross shape

Cylinder net: 2 circles + 1 rectangle (the rectangle's width = circumference of the circle)

Cone net: 1 circle + 1 sector (fan shape)

Triangular prism net: 2 triangles + 3 rectangles

3D Shapes Summary Table

Cube: 6 faces, 12 edges, 8 vertices | V = s³

Rectangular Prism: 6 faces, 12 edges, 8 vertices | V = lwh

Triangular Prism: 5 faces, 9 edges, 6 vertices | V = ½bh × length

Square Pyramid: 5 faces, 8 edges, 5 vertices | V = ⅓s²h

Cylinder: 3 faces, 2 edges, 0 vertices | V = πr²h

Sphere: 1 face, 0 edges, 0 vertices | V = (4/3)πr³

Cone: 2 faces, 1 edge, 1 vertex | V = ⅓πr²h

📏 Basic Trigonometry

The Pythagorean Theorem

Before we dive into trig ratios, you need to know the most famous theorem in math! In any right triangle:

a² + b² = c²

where c is the hypotenuse (the longest side, opposite the right angle) and a and b are the other two sides.

Example: A right triangle has legs a = 3 and b = 4. Find the hypotenuse.

c² = 3² + 4² = 9 + 16 = 25

c = √25 = 5 ✓

Example: Hypotenuse = 13, one leg = 5. Find the other leg.

a² + 5² = 13²

a² + 25 = 169

a² = 144

a = √144 = 12 ✓

🌍 Real world: A ladder leans against a wall. The ladder is 10 m long and the base is 6 m from the wall. How high up the wall does it reach?

h² + 6² = 10² → h² = 100 − 36 = 64 → h = 8 m

Common Pythagorean Triples

These are sets of whole numbers that satisfy a² + b² = c². Memorize them — they show up everywhere!

3, 4, 5

9 + 16 = 25

5, 12, 13

25 + 144 = 169

8, 15, 17

64 + 225 = 289

You can also scale these! If 3-4-5 works, so does 6-8-10, 9-12-15, 30-40-50, etc.

Right Triangles & Trig Ratios

Trigonometry studies the relationship between angles and sides in right triangles. For any angle θ (theta) in a right triangle, we label the sides relative to that angle:

SOH CAH TOA — The Three Ratios

This is the most important thing to remember in trigonometry:

SOH

sin θ = Opposite / Hypotenuse

CAH

cos θ = Adjacent / Hypotenuse

TOA

tan θ = Opposite / Adjacent

Memory trick: "Some Old Horse Caught A Horse Taking Oats Away" — the first letters spell SOH CAH TOA!

Finding Trig Ratios — Worked Examples

For angle θ in the triangle above:

Opposite = 6, Adjacent = 8, Hypotenuse = 10

sin θ = 6/10 = 0.6

cos θ = 8/10 = 0.8

tan θ = 6/8 = 0.75

Example 2: A right triangle has opposite = 5, hypotenuse = 13.

sin θ = 5/13 ≈ 0.385

First find adjacent using Pythagorean theorem: a = √(13² − 5²) = √(144) = 12

cos θ = 12/13 ≈ 0.923

tan θ = 5/12 ≈ 0.417

Special Triangles

Two triangles appear so often that you should memorize their ratios:

45°-45°-90° Triangle

Sides are in ratio 1 : 1 : √2

sin 45° = cos 45° = 1/√2 ≈ 0.707

tan 45° = 1

30°-60°-90° Triangle

Sides are in ratio 1 : √3 : 2

sin 30° = 1/2 = 0.5

cos 30° = √3/2 ≈ 0.866

tan 30° = 1/√3 ≈ 0.577

Using Trig to Find Missing Sides

If you know one angle and one side, you can find any other side!

Example: A right triangle has angle 35° and hypotenuse 20 cm. Find the opposite side.

We know the angle and hypotenuse, and want the opposite → use sin

sin 35° = opposite / 20

opposite = 20 × sin 35° = 20 × 0.574 ≈ 11.47 cm

Example: Angle = 50°, adjacent side = 12 cm. Find the opposite side.

We know adjacent and want opposite → use tan

tan 50° = opposite / 12

opposite = 12 × tan 50° = 12 × 1.192 ≈ 14.30 cm

How to choose which ratio:

1. Label the sides as Opposite, Adjacent, Hypotenuse relative to the known angle

2. Identify which two sides are involved (one known, one unknown)

3. Pick the ratio that uses those two sides: SOH, CAH, or TOA

Real-World Applications

🏔️ Height of a tree: You stand 15 m from a tree. The angle from the ground to the top of the tree is 40°. How tall is the tree?

tan 40° = height / 15

height = 15 × tan 40° = 15 × 0.839 ≈ 12.59 m

✈️ Airplane: A plane flies at an altitude of 3000 m. The angle of depression to the airport is 12°. How far is the plane from the airport (ground distance)?

tan 12° = 3000 / distance

distance = 3000 / tan 12° = 3000 / 0.213 ≈ 14,107 m (about 14.1 km)

🪜 Ladder safety: A 6 m ladder should make a 75° angle with the ground for safety. How high up the wall will it reach?

sin 75° = height / 6

height = 6 × sin 75° = 6 × 0.966 ≈ 5.80 m

Key Trig Values to Know

sin 0° = 0  |  sin 30° = 0.5  |  sin 45° ≈ 0.707  |  sin 60° ≈ 0.866  |  sin 90° = 1

cos 0° = 1  |  cos 30° ≈ 0.866  |  cos 45° ≈ 0.707  |  cos 60° = 0.5  |  cos 90° = 0

tan 0° = 0  |  tan 30° ≈ 0.577  |  tan 45° = 1  |  tan 60° ≈ 1.732  |  tan 90° = undefined

Notice: sin and cos are always between 0 and 1 (for angles 0°–90°). Tan can be any positive number!

Also notice: sin θ = cos(90° − θ). For example, sin 30° = cos 60° = 0.5. They are complementary!
